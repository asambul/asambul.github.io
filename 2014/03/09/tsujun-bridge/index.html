Tsujun Bridge

Tsūjun bridge is an arch bridge made of stones, which is located in Yamato town, Kumamoto Prefecture. The bridge is 84-meter long with the arch spans 27.3 meter. The Japanese Agency for Cultural Affairs has designated the bridge an Important Cultural Property. The bridge is so famous because it proves the high level of stone bridge technology at the time it was built.

Tsūjun bridge was built in 1854 by Yasunosuke Futa, an architect and also a chief of Yabe village, in Higo province. He wanted to open new rice fields. But the challenge was, he needed to find a way to convey water between two high places separated by a deep valley. By observing a rotten rainwater pipe which blew out water, he got an idea to build an aqueduct bridge. Inside the bridge, there are three aqueducts made from stones to transfer water from one high place to another, across the valley. They were made by stone because the water pressure was too high for wood. This makes Tsūjun bridge the largest stone aqueduct in Japan. Special mortar was developed to make the waterways tight and avoiding the leak. With the help of the group of 41 stone technicians and many farmers, the bridge was completed in about one year. It was said that, on the day of completion, Futa stood in a spot where, if the bridge failed, he would die.

The aqueduct system in Tsūjun bridge works according to the principle of inverted siphon. In this system, water is flowed to form a "U" shaped path. Water flowing in one end simply forces water up and out at the other end. This mechanism can be explained using the illustration below:

As long as there is a height difference between A and B, water will continuously flow from A to B through the U-shape aqueduct.

Because of it's shape, the sand or mud could accumulate in the lowest point of the aqueducts. That is why, two hole were constructed at the center of the bridge to clean out the aqueducts from such kind of sediments while discharging the water. When the holes are opened once in a while, the water pour out from both sides of the bridge and creating two giant arches of water (See picture below). It is a great show for tourists and became one of the appealing attractions of Tsūjun bridge.
